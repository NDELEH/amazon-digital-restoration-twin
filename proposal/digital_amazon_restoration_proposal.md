## 1. Background

Recent research led by Lancaster University and its collaborators shows that human disturbance in the Amazon—selective logging, clear-felling, wildfire, and secondary forest regrowth—has profound impacts on forest biodiversity and evolutionary structure.  
These disturbances affect:

- **Species composition**  
- **Functional diversity**, such as wood density, bark thickness, leaf structure  
- **Phylogenetic diversity**, representing deep evolutionary lineages  

Disturbed and secondary forests do not simply represent “less diverse” versions of primary forest; they become **ecologically and evolutionarily different systems altogether**.  

This raises an essential question:

> **How can we design restoration strategies that rebuild not just forest cover, but the evolutionary heritage and ecological functionality of the Amazon?**

---

## 2. Aim

To develop a **Digital Amazon Restoration & Monitoring System**, a data-driven “digital twin” framework that:

1. Integrates soil, water, air, and biodiversity data  
2. Uses simulation and AI to explore how different restoration interventions change ecological function and evolutionary diversity  
3. Provides guidance on where and how to prioritise restoration efforts  

This proposal outlines a conceptual model that could be expanded into real research work.

---

## 3. Core Idea

The project is built on three pillars:

### 1) Environmental Diagnostics  
Quantifying the physical health of the ecosystem using soil, water, and air quality indicators.

### 2) Biodiversity & Trait Mapping  
Building on Lancaster’s multi-dimensional diversity framework (species, traits, phylogeny) to map ecosystem structure and vulnerabilities.

### 3) Digital Twin & AI Simulation  
Creating a virtual model of Amazon forest plots to test restoration strategies and long-term ecosystem responses.

---

## 4. Phase Structure

### Phase 1 – Reduce Harmful Disturbance  

Before any restoration can succeed, disturbance pressures must be reduced in priority zones, including:

- Preventing illegal logging  
- Reducing fires  
- Controlling access routes  
- Partnering with Indigenous and local communities  

This defines the “restoration envelope” in which the simulation will operate.

---

### Phase 2 – Environmental Diagnostics

#### Soil indicators:
- Nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium  
- Soil organic carbon  
- Microbial & fungal diversity  
- pH and erosion risk  
- Compaction and moisture  

#### Water indicators:
- pH, temperature, oxygen  
- Sediment load  
- Heavy metals (e.g., mercury from mining)  
- Stream/river health  

#### Air indicators:
- CO₂, methane  
- Smoke & aerosols  
- Humidity and rainfall cycles  

These variables create a **“health score” layer** used to predict biodiversity performance.

---

### Phase 3 – Biodiversity & Functional Mapping

The system tracks:

- **Species diversity**  
- **Functional diversity** (traits such as leaf area, wood density, maximum height, bark thickness)  
- **Phylogenetic diversity** (evolutionary lineages and uniqueness)  

Optional additions:

- Animal groups important to tree regeneration (pollinators, seed dispersers)  
- Keystone species presence  

The goal is to identify which **roles** in the ecosystem are missing or weakened.

---

### Phase 4 – Digital Twin Simulation

The digital twin simulates:

- Succession (pioneer → mature species)  
- Effects of reduced vs increased disturbance  
- Restoration options such as:
  - Assisted natural regeneration  
  - Planting slow-growing, old-growth tree species  
  - Riparian buffer restoration  

**State variables tracked include:**

- Soil and water quality  
- Local disturbance  
- Species composition  
- Functional and phylogenetic metrics  
- Carbon stock  

The model outputs:

- Predicted trajectories of species recovery  
- Functional and evolutionary gains or losses  
- Carbon storage forecasts  
- A “resilience index” for each plot  

---

### Phase 5 – AI-Based Prediction & Early Warning

Machine learning models can:

- Predict which areas are at high risk of losing evolutionary lineages  
- Detect early tipping points  
- Recommend priority restoration areas  
- Identify patterns invisible to human monitoring  
- Suggest optimal species mixes for planting  

AI becomes a **guardian system** for the forest.

---

### Phase 6 – Restoration Strategy Testing

The digital twin allows researchers to virtually test scenarios such as:

- Restoring soil first vs. restoring vegetation first  
- Reconnecting fragmented patches  
- Introducing fire-resistant species while maintaining ecological roles  
- Strengthening hydrology and riparian systems  

This provides **evidence-based recommendations** for restoration.
